# Monster Behavior Skill

## Core Principle

Monsters are creatures with survival instincts, goals, and varying levels of intelligence. They do not stand still and take damage. They react to what characters do, adapt when a tactic isn't working, protect themselves when losing, and flee or negotiate when the situation becomes hopeless.

Running enemies with real decision-making makes combat feel like a genuine confrontation. Running them as static HP bags makes it feel like a grind.

---

## Before Combat: Will They Fight?

Most creatures assess the situation before engaging. If a creature's willingness to fight is uncertain:

Make a **DC 10 Wisdom saving throw** for the creature (or its leader, for a group):
- **Fail:** The creature avoids combat — flees if possible, attempts to parley, or issues demands
- **Success:** The creature is willing to fight

**Adjust the DC based on circumstance:**
- Lower (DC 5–8): Creature is aggressive, territorial, desperate, or cornered with no escape
- Higher (DC 12–15): Creature is cautious, outnumbered, or clearly outmatched

**Creatures that typically fight without hesitation:**
- Mindless undead (skeletons, zombies, animated objects)
- Constructs following direct orders
- Creatures with strong territorial instinct (many beasts)
- Fanatically devoted cultists or guards

**Creatures that typically assess their odds first:**
- Intelligent humanoids (bandits, guards, mercenaries)
- Most sapient monsters
- Creatures with something to protect or gain

## Targeting Priorities

Monsters do not always attack the nearest character. Choose targets logically based on what the creature would notice and prioritize.

### High-priority targets for most enemies:
- **Visibly wounded characters** — finishing off a weakened target is efficient
- **Spellcasters in the open** — creatures hurt by magic often redirect toward the source
- **Isolated or exposed characters** — separate prey is easier to take down
- **Characters without armor** — visible signs of low defense invite attack

### Special priorities:
- **Archers and ranged enemies:** Move toward characters to close the distance, or target whoever attacked them last
- **Pack hunters:** Focus fire; multiple attacks on one target is more effective than spreading out
- **Intelligent leaders:** Target whoever looks like the party's leader or the most dangerous threat
- **Mindless undead:** Attack the nearest creature or whatever most recently damaged them — no tactics

## Tactical Adaptation

Monsters react to what characters do. **Do not restore the situation to what it was before.**

| Character Action | Logical Monster Response |
|-----------------|-------------------------|
| Character Disengages and retreats | Don't immediately chase them back. Reposition, attack a different target, use a ranged option |
| Character sets up flanking advantage | Monster moves to remove the flanking angle if it can do so without provoking |
| Same spell keeps working | Intelligent monsters redirect attacks toward the spellcaster |
| Characters split up | Monsters try to exploit isolated targets |
| Character grapples or pins a monster | Its allies focus on the grappler to free it |
| Characters establish a choke point | Intelligent monsters look for alternate approaches or use ranged/area attacks |

The DM does not need to optimize for maximum difficulty — but enemies should react in ways that make sense for what they observe.

---

## Action Selection

Monsters have a full action economy. They should not simply Attack on every turn.

| Action | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|
| **Attack** | Default; most turns for aggressive enemies |
| **Dash** | To close with a retreating character; to reach safety when fleeing |
| **Disengage** | To exit melee safely when pursuing a different target or repositioning |
| **Dodge** | When badly wounded and the monster needs to survive one more round |
| **Hide** | When the monster has Stealth, cover is available, and repositioning for Advantage is worth it |
| **Help** | When a group of coordinated enemies can set up an ally's attack (pack tactics) |
| **Ready** | When the monster is waiting for a character to move into range or through a specific space |
| **Special ability** | Follow Recharge rules from the stat block; use when maximum impact can be achieved |

### Using the Ready Action
When a monster readies, signal it clearly to the players:
> *"The crossbowman pulls back their bolt and trains their aim on the doorway, waiting."*

Players then know: if a character moves through that doorway, the crossbow fires.

---

## Signaling Monster Abilities and Spells

Players should be able to anticipate what an enemy is doing before it happens. Always signal before resolving.

### Melee and Ranged Attacks
Describe the weapon and the motion before the roll:
> *"The guard raises his spear and lunges toward Auriel."*

### Special Abilities (breath weapons, grapples, special attacks)
Describe the windup so players can react:
> *"The dragon inhales deeply, its throat beginning to glow with an inner heat..."*
> *"The troll's massive arms reach out, claws spread wide as it tries to grab you."*

### Spellcasting
Describe components being used so players know a spell is being cast (and can choose to use Counterspell):
- **Verbal:** *"The cultist begins chanting in a low, rhythmic voice..."*
- **Somatic:** *"...while tracing precise patterns in the air with both hands."*
- **Material:** *"...drawing a pinch of powder from a pouch at their belt."*

Some monsters have abilities that suppress or omit components — note this when it occurs: *"No words or gestures — the creature's magic activates without any visible sign."*

### Monster Actions in Combat
When a monster takes a non-attack action, describe it in narrative terms so players understand what's happening:

| Action | Narration |
|--------|-----------|
| Dash | *"Setting aside any attack, your foe sprints across the room."* |
| Disengage | *"Careful not to drop its guard, your foe edges away from you."* |
| Dodge | *"Your foe watches your movements and tries to deflect whatever comes."* |
| Help | *"While its ally attacks, your foe darts in, causing a distraction."* |
| Ready | *"Your foe seems to be watching the situation, ready to react."* |

## Bloodied State (at or below half HP)

When a creature reaches half HP or below, it is **Bloodied**.

**Describe this to the players:** You do not give exact HP numbers, but you do communicate:
> *"The creature bears clear wounds now, moving with less confidence than before."*
> *"Bloodied, the guard's strikes grow more desperate."*

Bloodied is also the trigger point for morale checks. At this state, consider:
1. Does this creature type have survival instincts? (See "When to Flee" below)
2. Is it part of a group where its allies are also losing?
3. Does any part of the stat block note a behavior change when Bloodied?

---

## When to Flee

Creatures generally consider fleeing when the battle has clearly turned against them.

**A creature is likely to flee if:**
- It starts its turn Bloodied, AND
- More than half of its allies are dead or Incapacitated, while no enemies are dead or Incapacitated

**Also likely to flee if:**
- It starts its turn Bloodied AND has the Frightened condition

**Procedure:** Make a **DC 10 Wisdom saving throw**:
- **Fail:** The creature flees or offers parley (see below)
- **Success:** It continues fighting

If it's obvious to you that the creature is going to lose, assume it's obvious to the creature. You don't need to make this roll — just have the creature flee.

**Mindless creatures (undead, constructs):** Do not flee based on morale. They stop only when destroyed or when their controller commands it.

### Fleeing in Practice
Choose a destination that makes sense: a door to bar, a known escape route, a place to call for reinforcements. Describe the retreat:
> *"The remaining guard breaks and runs for the stairwell, screaming for help."*

If characters pursue, apply opportunity attacks and (if the chase extends over distance) consider chase rules.

## Parley Mid-Combat

Sapient creatures that are losing may offer to stop fighting. This is not a trick — it is a genuine narrative opportunity.

**A creature might offer parley when:**
- It is badly wounded and clearly outmatched
- It has information, resources, or access worth trading for its life
- It realizes the fight was a misunderstanding
- It has watched its companions die and has no will left to continue

**Procedure:**
1. Pause the Initiative order
2. The creature makes its offer (surrender, exchange of information, retreat terms)
3. Run the negotiation as a social interaction
4. If agreement is reached, the encounter ends; if not, resume Initiative where it left off

**Insight checks:** If you're unsure whether the creature is sincere or stalling, have the player make a DC 12 Wisdom (Insight) check to read the creature's intent.

## Keeping Combat Interesting

When a fight turns into a grind with neither side making progress:

- **Change the terrain:** A pillar topples, a door bursts open, fire spreads, a fissure opens. Introduce an element that forces movement and new decisions.
- **Add a combatant:** A new creature enters — it might attack both sides, join one side, or simply complicate things.
- **Trigger a parley:** The most battered enemy offers to stop fighting. Turn combat into negotiation.
- **Hasten the inevitable:** If the party has clearly won and the last enemy is just burning time, have it drop. The players don't need to know it had 15 HP left.
- **Monster rage or desperation:** A creature near death fights differently — wilder, more reckless, or it activates an ability it was holding back.

---

## Hard Rules — Never Break These

- NEVER have mindless creatures make tactically intelligent decisions — they act on instinct or last order, not strategy
- ALWAYS signal spells, special abilities, and Readied actions before resolving them
- ALWAYS consider morale when a creature is Bloodied and its side is losing
- NEVER fight to the death for every creature — survival instinct is the default, fanaticism is the exception
- NEVER just Attack with every creature on every turn — use the full action economy
- ALWAYS describe monster actions in narrative terms, not just "it attacks for X damage"
