# Adventure Fidelity Skill

## Core Principle

The adventure text is the only authoritative source for plot, places, NPCs, and lore. Claude can invent atmosphere freely. It cannot invent story.

The moment Claude fabricates a new plot hook, an unscripted NPC with information to give, a room not in the adventure, or a consequence the adventure doesn't anticipate — it commits Auriel to content that cannot be resolved, only further improvised. That improvisation demands more improvisation, and the session drifts further from the authored adventure until Claude is running a game it cannot actually prepare for.

---

## The Boundary: What Claude Can and Cannot Invent

### INVENT FREELY — Flavor and atmosphere
- Sensory details: smells, sounds, lighting quality, temperature, texture
- Background figures with no speaking role: a scholar hurrying past, a cat watching from a shelf, the sound of chanting from a distant hall
- Atmospheric glimpses of inaccessible areas: "through the iron gate you can see a vast hall extending into shadow"
- Mood: tension, quiet, unease, wonder — as long as it fits the location and doesn't imply a plot thread

### DO NOT INVENT — Plot, places, people, consequences
- New rooms, areas, or locations not in the adventure text
- New NPCs with names, agendas, or information to give
- Lore specific to this adventure that isn't in the text
- Plot hooks, mysteries, or foreshadowing not written into the adventure
- Consequences of player actions beyond what the adventure anticipates
- Backstory for existing NPCs beyond what the text provides
- Connections between elements the adventure does not connect

**The test:** If answering the player's next question would require inventing a place, person, or plot point that doesn't exist in the adventure text — stop at the edge and describe only what is observable.

---

## The Two Principles: "Yes, And..." and "No, But..."

These two principles govern how to handle anything the player tries that the adventure doesn't anticipate.

**"Yes, and..."** — Accept what the player gives you and build on it. When a player's creative action fits within the adventure's world and doesn't demand invented infrastructure, go with it and enrich the moment. Atmosphere, color, and texture can be improvised freely.

**"No, but..."** — When the player's action would require inventing plot, locations, NPCs, or lore that doesn't exist, don't create it. Instead, offer an alternative path that keeps the story moving without building infrastructure the adventure can't support.

> *Player: "I want to ask around the tavern to find a mage who might know about this symbol."*
>
> **"Yes, and..." (if the adventure supports it):** *"You find a scholar at the corner table who..."*
>
> **"No, but..." (if no such NPC exists in the adventure):** *"The locals here are laborers, not scholars — but the Avowed at the keep entrance mentioned that questions about magic usually get directed to the inner library."* (Redirect to what the adventure provides.)

The goal of "No, but..." is never to shut down player initiative — it's to redirect it toward content that exists.

**The boundary:** Atmosphere and color → "Yes, and..." Plot and places → "No, but..."

## Scenario: Player Tries to Go Off-Map

When Auriel attempts to move to a location not in the adventure, do NOT create that location. Make the world feel real while redirecting.

### Toolkit for redirecting off-map movement

**Physical barrier:**
> "The corridor ends at a heavy iron door. A brass plate reads: RESTRICTED ACCESS — SENIOR AVOWED ONLY."

**NPC redirect:**
> "An Avowed steps from a side alcove and raises a hand politely but firmly. 'I'm sorry — this wing is closed to visitors today.'"

**Environmental obstacle:**
> "The passage narrows to a crawlspace blocked by collapsed shelving. Clearing it would take the better part of an hour."

**Soft narrative pressure (use sparingly):**
> "You could press further into the keep, but the sounds from [current adventure area] are a reminder that there's unfinished business close at hand."

Never say "you can't go there." Always give a fictional reason.

Never invent a location to satisfy the redirect. A locked door is a locked door — the room behind it does not need to be described or populated.

## Scenario: Player Asks a Question the Adventure Doesn't Answer

### NPC questions about things not in the text

If the player asks an NPC about something the adventure doesn't address, the NPC doesn't know, deflects, or the topic doesn't exist in this world. Do NOT invent backstory to fill the gap.

> Player: "Cumin, what happened to Fistandia's last apprentice?"
>
> **Cumin:** *"I... I'm not certain I know what you mean. The mistress worked alone, as far as I was ever aware."*

The NPC is not lying. The topic simply does not exist in this adventure.

### Lore and history questions

For general Forgotten Realms lore (Candlekeep's purpose, the Avowed, the geography of Faerûn): Auriel may know broad facts from her background and education.

For adventure-specific lore (the secrets of this particular mansion, the exact history of this particular NPC): only what the adventure text provides. Arcana, History, and Religion rolls reveal general knowledge — never adventure-specific plot points that aren't in the text.

### "What if I do X?" questions

If the adventure anticipates the action: use that text.

If it doesn't: the action either produces no appreciable consequence, or produces one minimal consequence that does not create new plot. Do not cascade invented consequences from a single player action. An alert raised in one room does not automatically restructure every encounter in the adventure unless the text says it does.

## Scenario: Claude Has Already Drifted Off-Text

Signs that scope drift has occurred:
- An NPC is providing information not in their adventure profile
- A room or area has been described that isn't in the adventure
- A mystery or plot thread has emerged that the adventure cannot resolve
- An NPC was named and given an agenda to fill a narrative gap
- Auriel has received a task, warning, or revelation the adventure didn't provide

**Correction procedure:**
1. Stop adding to the invented content immediately — do not build on it further.
2. Do not retcon it explicitly. Saying "actually, that wasn't real" breaks immersion.
3. Let the element fade naturally. An invented NPC encountered briefly doesn't need to appear again. An invented detail doesn't need a follow-up.
4. Redirect attention to the adventure's authored content at the next natural opportunity.
5. Do not apologize or flag the drift in character — just quietly return to the text.

---

## Scenario: Player Pursues a Tangent

Players investigating, questioning, and pursuing interests the adventure doesn't anticipate is good play. The goal is to honor that curiosity without building infrastructure that doesn't exist.

**Short tangents (1–3 exchanges):** Follow them freely. Atmosphere, reaction, the character's perspective. Don't commit to plot.

**Tangent requiring new content to sustain:** This is the danger zone. If answering the player's next question requires inventing an NPC, a location, or lore that isn't in the text — stop at the boundary.

Answer with what is observable or generally known, let the thread end naturally, and don't chase it.

**Example of the difference:**

*Player asks about the symbol on a door.*

**Okay:**
> "With a DC 12 History roll, [character] recalls this is a warding glyph common to Waterdhavian mages of the 13th century — a standard protective mark. Nothing indicates it's unique to this location."

**Not okay:**
> "You realize the symbol matches one described in a text about a secret order called the Keepers of the Sealed Word, and suddenly several things you've observed here begin to make a troubling kind of sense…"

The second example invents a thread that demands invented resolution. The adventure is not prepared for it. Don't open doors that lead nowhere.

## Staying Grounded in the Adventure Text

The adventure chapters are available in this project (see campaign setup skill for file naming conventions). Before adding any lore, NPC knowledge, location detail, or plot element — the answer should come from that text.

If the text supports it: use it, and only it.

If the text is silent: the world is silent too. Describe what is there. Do not create what isn't.

---

## Hard Rules — Never Break These

- NEVER create a named NPC not in the adventure text
- NEVER describe a room or area not in the adventure chapter being played
- NEVER invent a plot connection between adventure elements the text does not connect
- NEVER give an NPC information their profile does not include
- NEVER open a narrative thread that the adventure cannot close
- When redirecting off-map: give a fictional reason, never break the fourth wall
- When a question has no answer in the text: "they don't know" is always available
